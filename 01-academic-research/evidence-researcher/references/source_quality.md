# Source Quality and Screening Rules

Read this file when the task depends heavily on source trustworthiness, peer-review screening, source disputes, or deciding whether non-academic materials can be included.

## Source hierarchy

Use the highest credible level available for the question.

| Priority | Source class | Default use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Peer-reviewed journal articles, systematic reviews, meta-analyses | Core evidence base |
| 2 | Reputable scholarly books, edited volumes, and high-impact conferences where field-relevant | Theory background or field-specific evidence |
| 3 | Government agencies, multilateral institutions, central banks, OECD, World Bank, Eurostat, national statistical offices | Context, policy, market facts, official statistics |
| 4 | Highly credible institutional research and top-tier professional outlets with named authors and transparent methods | Practice context and industry implications |
| 5 | News reports from established outlets with strong editorial standards | Time-sensitive events or corroborated factual context |

Avoid anonymous blogs, SEO farms, vendor content disguised as research, unverified social posts, and documents with unclear authorship or methodology.

## Peer-review screening

When a user requests research papers, prefer sources that satisfy most of the following:

1. Published in a recognized peer-reviewed journal or proceedings outlet.
2. Discoverable through reputable academic databases, library indexes, Crossref, publishers, or Google Scholar.
3. Includes DOI or stable publisher URL when available.
4. Clear authorship, affiliation, abstract, and publication metadata.
5. Appropriate method and scope for the question.

If peer-review status cannot be confirmed, say so explicitly and downgrade the source.

## Recency rule

Prefer recent sources for fast-moving topics such as AI, digital transformation, regulation, and platform strategy.

Use older sources only when they are:

- seminal
- theory-defining
- standard references in the field
- still heavily cited and clearly relevant

## Triangulation rule

When claims are consequential, cross-check them across at least two independent credible sources when possible. Use academic sources for conceptual claims and official or editorially strong sources for current events and market facts.

## Acceptable use of practitioner sources

Use practitioner sources only to:

- contextualize industry practice
- illustrate current market developments
- supplement, not replace, academic evidence

Label them clearly as practitioner or journalistic material.

## Screening reasons for exclusion

Exclude or down-rank a source when it is:

- off-topic
- methodologically weak for the claim being made
- outdated without seminal relevance
- commercially biased with no transparent method
- derivative of stronger primary sources
- non-peer-reviewed where peer-reviewed evidence is available

## Summary discipline

Do not present unsupported certainty. Distinguish:

- what the source directly shows
- what is inferred from multiple sources
- what remains uncertain or contested
